Chapter 01

The core concept — why emotion sells

Traditional e-commerce assumes customers know what they want. They land on a product grid, scan items, compare prices, and maybe buy one thing. The average order on a browse-first store is $31.

FeelSpace starts from a completely different premise: people don't buy decor. They buy how a space makes them feel. When someone thinks "I want to update my entryway for fall," they're not thinking "I need a 22-inch maple leaf wreath." They're thinking "I want warmth. I want it to feel welcoming. I want autumn to feel like home."

"The most powerful thing you can sell is a feeling — and the way you sell it is to show it first, then reveal the products that create it."

FeelSpace operationalizes this insight. A customer types a mood. The platform generates a complete, designed entryway scene with all the products placed contextually. The customer doesn't shop — they just say yes. Average basket: $127.

Why entryways are the perfect starting point

The entryway wedge is strategic, not accidental. Entryways are:

The billion-dollar insight

The home decor market is $130B. Less than 3% of it is sold with context — meaning products shown as part of a designed environment. FeelSpace is building the infrastructure to make contextual, emotion-first commerce the default.

Chapter 02

Getting started — your first 72 hours

The fastest path to revenue is a focused MVP: one season, one mood category, 30–50 carefully curated SKUs. Here's exactly how to spend your first three days.

Day 1 — Account and catalog setup

1
Create your FeelSpace account
Start on the free Seed plan. You can upgrade to Grow ($149/mo) once your first drop is live and converting.
2
Choose your launch season and mood
Don't try to launch all seasons at once. Pick the one that's coming next. Fall is historically the highest-converting season for entryways.
3
Upload your catalog
Start with 30–50 products maximum. Each product needs: name, price, clear product photo, category (wreath / planter / lighting / mat / decor / furniture). That's it.
4
Review AI emotion tags
After upload, the system auto-tags each product with emotional qualities (warm, cozy, festive, minimal, natural, etc.). Spot-check these — they determine which scenes your products appear in.

Day 2 — Scene creation

5
Generate your first 5 scenes
Use the quick moods as starting points. Each scene should feel distinct — don't generate five versions of "warm autumn." Try "warm," "cozy," "festive," "minimal," and "vibrant."
6
Tune each scene with the slider controls
The warmth, lushness, and natural sliders change which products appear. A high-lush scene will pull more plants; a minimal scene will surface simpler textures.
7
Verify product placement makes sense
Manually review each scene. If a product feels wrong (a tropical planter in a winter scene, for example), override the tag in your catalog settings.

Day 3 — Your first drop

8
Bundle 3–5 scenes into a named Drop
Give it a mood name: "The Autumn Calm Drop." Write a 2-sentence description focused entirely on feeling, not products.
9
Set a drop window (5–7 days works best)
Scarcity drives decisions. "Available until Sunday" converts better than "always available."
10
Share your drop launch post
Use the built-in share card generator. The card should lead with the scene visual, not the product list. Let the emotion sell first.
Common mistake to avoid

Don't launch with 200 products and 20 scenes hoping the AI figures it out. A tight, curated first drop with 5 scenes and 40 products will convert at 3× the rate of an overwhelming catalog. Quality of context beats quantity every time.

Chapter 03

Building your catalog — products that work in scenes

Not all products translate equally well to scene-based selling. Here's how to structure your catalog for maximum scene coverage and conversion.

The 5 product categories you need

Every high-converting entryway scene contains at least one product from each of these five categories. If you're missing a category, your scenes will feel incomplete and customers will sense it.

Category 01
The focal point
Wreaths, door signs, seasonal arrangements. This is the hero product — the item that defines the mood. Price: $35–$80. You need at least 3–4 options per season.
Category 02
Living elements
Planters, pots, florals, seasonal plants. These ground the scene and add organic warmth. Price: $25–$75. Seasonal variety matters here — mums for fall, tulips for spring.
Category 03
Lighting
Lanterns, candles, string lights. Lighting adds atmosphere and drives basket value. Price: $20–$65. Even minimal scenes benefit from a single lantern.
Category 04
Ground layer
Doormats, rugs, entry runners. The lowest price point but high attach rate — customers almost always add one. Price: $25–$55. Offer 2–3 texture options per mood.
Category 05
Accent objects
Vases, benches, baskets, decorative stakes. These fill in scenes and push basket size. Price: $20–$150. One or two per mood is enough — don't over-populate.

Writing product descriptions that work with the AI

The emotion engine reads your product descriptions to assign tags. Write descriptions that include feeling words, not just features. Compare:

Description that tags poorly

"Maple leaf wreath, 22 inches, wire frame, hand-dried leaves, natural colors."

Description that tags well

"Hand-dried maple leaf wreath — rich autumn tones of rust, amber, and burgundy. Warm, welcoming, and deeply seasonal. 22-inch diameter, natural wire frame. Perfect for creating that cozy fall first impression."

The second description gives the emotion engine warmth, autumn, cozy, welcoming, and seasonal. It will appear in 3× more scene types.

Chapter 04

Creating your first scene — the craft of emotional composition

Generating a scene takes seconds. Making a scene that converts takes a bit more craft. Here's how to think about scene quality.

The anatomy of a high-converting scene

The best scenes share four qualities: emotional coherence (everything feels like it belongs together), seasonal specificity (nothing is generic), aspirational but achievable (customers can imagine their home looking like this), and visual balance (not too sparse, not too cluttered).

Scene naming — this matters more than you think

The scene name is the first thing customers see and share. Bad names describe products. Good names describe feelings.

Bad scene nameGood scene nameWhy it works
Fall Wreath BundleAutumn CalmSells a state of being, not a set of objects
Winter Decor SetFrost & GlowCreates a sensory picture in two words
Spring ProductsFirst LightEvokes a specific moment, not a season
Minimal EntrySunday QuietGives the customer a character to inhabit

Iterating with the scene composer

Once your base scene is generated, use natural language to refine it. Effective prompts:

// These work well "Make it warmer and more textured" "Add more greenery, less hardscape" "I want it to feel like a Sunday morning, not a holiday" "More rustic, less polished" "What would this look like in a coastal home?" // These don't work as well "Add product SKU-4421" // Too product-focused "Make it cheaper" // The engine doesn't optimize for price
Chapter 05

Seasonal drops strategy — scarcity + emotion = urgency

Drops are the highest-leverage growth mechanism in FeelSpace. A well-executed drop creates urgency, generates social sharing, and drives repeat purchase behavior. Here's how to plan a full year of drops.

Annual drop calendar

🍂
Early September — The First Fall Drop
Launch 3–4 weeks before peak fall demand. Mood: warm, transitional, "summer ending." Lead with dried botanicals and muted terracottas. This drop educates your customer on the scene-buying experience.
🎃
Mid-October — Peak Autumn Drop
Your biggest volume drop of the year. More festive, deeper autumn palette. Introduce pumpkins and warmer lighting. Price this drop slightly higher — customers are in buying mode.
❄️
Late November — First Winter Drop
The transition drop. Mix late autumn with early winter — cedar, white pines, warm metallics. Mood: "cozy indoors" rather than "festive." Holds well through December 1st.
🎄
December — The Holiday Drop
Fully festive — reds, deep greens, frosted whites. This is a gifting drop. Your scene share cards become gift ideas. Highest average basket of the year (~$180+).
🌿
February — Winter Refresh Drop
The January lull is real. A February drop with "calm, minimal, natural" moods performs well — customers are tired of holiday and want quiet.
🌸
April — Spring Awakening Drop
Second biggest drop of the year. Wildflowers, pastels, fresh citrus. Mood: relief, brightness, newness. Customers are ready to spend after a long winter.
Drop copy formula that works

Subject line: [Feeling] is here. [Drop name] — available for [X] days.

Opening line: That feeling when [specific sensory moment]. That's what we built this drop around.

Body: Describe the mood in 2–3 sentences. Name 2–3 products. End with the close window.

Example: "That feeling when you open the front door on a cool October morning and everything smells like woodsmoke and dried leaves. That's the Autumn Calm Drop. Available for 6 days."

Chapter 06

The creator economy layer — distribution you don't have to pay for

The creator layer is the highest-leverage growth mechanism after drops. A single mid-size creator (20k–100k followers) publishing a scene can generate $3k–$15k in attributed revenue — at zero acquisition cost to you.

Who makes a good FeelSpace creator?

You're not looking for influencers with the biggest audiences. You're looking for people whose aesthetic already aligns with your catalog. The right creator has:

○
Genuine home content — they post about their own space, not just products
○
Seasonal awareness — they already decorate for seasons and share it
○
Engaged small audience — 5k engaged followers > 500k passive ones
○
They describe feelings, not specs — they say "cozy" and "welcoming" not "on-trend"
○
Their audience shops for their home — not just fashion, travel, or food

Creator onboarding flow

1
Invite to platform
Give creators a free Grow-tier account. They need full scene generation to publish good content.
2
They create named mood scenes
Creators publish scenes under their name: "Sunday Garden Welcome by @SageHomeStudio." Their scenes use your catalog products as the shoppable layer.
3
Followers browse and buy
Followers click through to the creator's scene storefront. All purchases from that scene are attributed to the creator.
4
Creator earns 15% revenue share
Paid monthly. No minimums. The more scenes they publish and share, the more they earn.

Chapter 08

Scaling roadmap — from entryway to entire home

Phase 1 · Months 1–3
Entryway wedge
Focus exclusively on entryways. Perfect your scene generation, nail 3–4 seasonal drops, recruit your first 5 creators. Prove the emotion-to-basket thesis before expanding.
Phase 2 · Months 4–6
Merchant infrastructure
Open the platform to other home & garden merchants. They pay $149–$499/mo to plug their catalogs into your scene engine. You become a platform, not just a retailer.
Phase 3 · Months 7–12
Creator marketplace
Build the creator storefront. Top creators get profile pages. Their followers can browse all their published scenes. This creates a discovery layer that drives organic traffic.
Phase 4 · Year 2
Room expansion
Expand from entryways to living rooms, then balconies, then bedrooms. Each new room category is a new market with the same emotion-first mechanic.
