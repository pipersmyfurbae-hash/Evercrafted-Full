Balance Reference

Symmetrical Balance

Mirror-image distribution across the vertical axis. Place equal-weight clusters at 10° and 170°, 2° and 178°. Feels formal, traditional, timeless.

Asymmetrical Balance

Unequal visual weight creating dynamic tension. A large focal cluster at 315° balanced by smaller rhythm clusters spanning 90°–180°. Evercrafted's default.

Radial Balance

Golden angle distribution (137.5°). Place each new element at +137.5° from the last. Produces natural, botanical-looking scatter across the full ring.

24" wreath · asymmetric layout · r_work = 9.5"

Rhythm Reference

Repetition

Use the same floral element at regular intervals. 3 identical clusters at 120° intervals creates a strong, resolved rhythm. Minimum 3 repetitions to register.

Alternation

A–B–A–B pattern. Alternate a focal flower with a texture element every 45°. Creates movement without chaos. Ideal for garland-style designs.

Progression

Scale or density increases along a direction. Start with 3 stems at 270°, grow to 7 stems by 315°. Creates visual momentum toward a focal point.

Flow Direction

All stems should reinforce a single dominant flow — clockwise or counter-clockwise. Conflicting stem directions read as chaos, not texture.

Focal Point Reference

Primary Focal Point

The single dominant element the eye lands on first. Place between 300°–330° (upper-left of ring). Use your largest, most dramatic bloom at r_work with ±10° spread. Max 1 per design.

Secondary Focal Points

Supporting anchors that create rhythm with the primary. Place at 180° and 90° offset from primary. 60–70% the scale of the primary. Max 2 per design.

Eye Path

The invisible line the viewer's eye travels. Primary → secondary → filler → back to primary. Design this path deliberately using size, color, and direction cues.

Color Theory Reference

60–30–10 Rule

60% dominant (greenery, base texture), 30% secondary (main florals), 10% accent (pops of contrast or metallic). Apply to stem count, not just visual area.

Warm / Cool Balance

Every palette needs both warm and cool tones. Pure warm palettes feel heavy; pure cool palettes feel cold. A 70/30 split reads most naturally to the eye.
